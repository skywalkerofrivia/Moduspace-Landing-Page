Acrylic vs Glass Display Cases:
Which Is Better for Collectors?

Read8 min
PublishedApril 2026
Paired withAll Moducases

The Green TintShatter RiskWeight & AssemblyThe Verdict

The first thing a glass case does to your figure is tint it. The second is wait for the day someone bumps it. Acrylic, done properly, removes both problems — and a few you didn't know you had.

The green tint nobody warned you about

Pick up a sheet of standard float glass and look at it from the edge. That faint olive-green cast is iron oxide, an unavoidable byproduct of the manufacturing process. Every photograph of a figure behind glass is filtered through that tint — subtly, but always.

Optical-grade acrylic, by contrast, transmits 92% of visible light without any color shift. The figure stays the color the sculptor finished it in.

One bump. Three hundred dollars.

A glass panel hit at the wrong angle doesn't crack — it explodes. We've replaced enough customer figures to know the math: a single panel failure on a tempered case can cost more than the case did.

Acrylic flexes. It scratches if you abuse it, and a deep gouge can be polished out by hand. It does not shower a figure in shards.

I had a glass shelf shatter onto a Predator bust. That was the day I switched the whole room.

Half the weight. Twice the lifespan.

A 600×400×500mm glass case weighs about 28 kg empty. The acrylic equivalent: 12 kg. That difference matters when you're stacking five high on a wall, mounting on drywall, or simply carrying the case up a flight of stairs after delivery.

When glass is the right call

We won't pretend it never is. If your collection lives in direct, prolonged sunlight and you can't relocate it, museum-grade UV glass outperforms standard acrylic on long-term yellowing. For everything else — acrylic wins on clarity, safety, weight, and serviceability.
