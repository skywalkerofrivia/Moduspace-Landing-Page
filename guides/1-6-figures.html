      How to Choose a Display Case
      for 1/6 Scale Figures

      Figures · 11 min read · April 2026 · Paired with Sixth Series
      Contents
      Understanding 1/6 dimensions The posing problem Case depth for action poses Lighting for fabric costumes Our recommendation

      A 1/6 scale figure isn't just a tall object. It's a posed object — arms extended, weapons drawn, capes sweeping — and the case you choose must accommodate not just the standing height but the full three-dimensional envelope of the pose you've chosen to display. Most collectors find this out after they've already bought the wrong case.

      Understanding 1/6 scale dimensions

      The "1/6 scale" designation means a figure is approximately one-sixth the height of a real person — which puts most figures in the 28–36cm range for body height. But body height is only part of the equation.

      Hot Toys figures in particular are engineered with remarkable anatomical accuracy, which means the shoulder width and arm span of a figure in a wide stance can easily extend to 40cm or more. A figure holding a rifle at port arms, or a figure with both arms raised, can need 50cm or more of interior horizontal clearance. A figure with a swept cape — any of the Batman variants, the Spider-Man figures with flowing fabric — needs depth as well as width.

      Figure type Typical height Recommended interior
      Standard standing pose28–32 cmH: 40cm, W: 30cm, D: 25cm
      Wide stance / combat pose30–36 cmH: 45cm, W: 45cm, D: 35cm
      Weapon extended / cape30–36 cmH: 45cm, W: 50cm, D: 40cm
      Diorama base included32–40 cmH: 55cm, W: 60cm, D: 45cm

      The standard Sixth Series case provides 480mm interior height, 380mm width, and 320mm depth per module — sized to accommodate the full posing range of virtually every 1/6 figure currently in production, including those with extended accessories, without requiring custom configurations for most manufacturers.

      The posing problem

      Here's what the dimension tables don't capture: you will change your mind about the pose. This is almost universal among serious 1/6 collectors. The figure arrives, you pose it one way, display it for six months, then revisit the posing options and decide you'd rather display the alternate head with the weapon holstered and the right arm raised.

      A case that precisely fitted your original pose now doesn't accommodate the new one. This is why the Sixth Series is sized with deliberate headroom — not to give your figure a swimming pool to rattle around in, but to give you the flexibility to pose freely without measuring against case constraints before every display decision.

      It also matters for multi-figure displays. Collectors who start with single figures often end up wanting to display pairs — Tony and Rhodey in their respective armours, or a hero-villain confrontation. These groupings require significantly more horizontal clearance than the sum of two individual figure widths, because the composition itself takes up space.

      "The case you buy should accommodate the collection you'll have in three years, not the pose you chose yesterday."

      Case depth for action poses

      Depth is the most underestimated dimension in figure display. Collectors who've never worked with 1/6 scale figures in a case often think about height and width — but the depth of the case determines what poses are actually displayable.

      A figure in a standard standing pose with arms at sides might need only 20–22cm of depth. The same figure in a walking pose, with one leg forward and the torso rotated, might need 30cm. A cape — especially a fabric cape with good drape — can extend 20–25cm behind the figure's back at rest. Put that figure on a raised base and you're looking at a total depth requirement of 40cm or more.

      Many collectors discover this after mounting a beautiful Hot Toys Batman in their existing case and finding that the cape doesn't drape — it's pressed flat against the back panel, taking a permanent set that destroys the sculptural quality of the fabric.

      The correct solution isn't to cut the case — it's to buy the right depth from the start. The Sixth Series standard depth of 320mm handles 95% of poses. For diorama bases and the largest fabric elements, the 400mm extended-depth configuration removes all compromises entirely.

      Lighting for fabric costumes

      1/6 scale figures are exceptional in one specific way: the textured surfaces that other scale figures fake with paint — fabric suits, leather textures, metal mesh — are actually present in physical form. Hot Toys figures wear real fabric. This is both their greatest quality and their most demanding lighting challenge.

      Overhead LED strips — the default option in most budget cases — flatten fabric texture. The light comes from above, fills the shadows in woven cloth, and what should read as luxurious textured leather reads as a flat painted surface. You lose the quality you paid for.

      The correct approach is angled front lighting: LEDs positioned at the front edge of the top of the case, angled down and in at approximately 30–45 degrees. This creates the grazing light that reveals surface texture, makes fabric look dimensional, and catches the micro-detail of weathering and paintwork that 1/6 manufacturers invest so much in.

      The Sixth Series Lighting Kit uses precisely this configuration — a front-mounted LED channel with adjustable angle and 2700K–5000K colour temperature control, so you can match the warmth of your room lighting or dial in a cooler, more dramatic tone for display.

      Our recommendation

      For most 1/6 collectors, the Sixth Series standard configuration handles the full range of display needs. Here's how to approach the decision:

      • Start with how many figures you plan to display — and add 30% for the ones you'll acquire after you buy the case.
      • Measure your most extreme existing pose — the widest stance, the most extended arm, the longest weapon — and confirm the case depth and width cover it with at least 40mm to spare on each side.
      • Plan for diorama bases now — if any of your figures sit on raised scenic bases, add the base height to the figure height and confirm the interior clearance is sufficient before ordering.
      • Order the lighting kit with the case — retrofitting lighting into an installed case is significantly more difficult than building it in from the start.
